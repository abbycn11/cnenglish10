Slavery in the American South

1 / 10

The System of Chattel Slavery

During the decades preceding the Civil War, the Southern United States built a highly lucrative agricultural empire fueled completely by enslaved labor. Enslaved persons had zero legal protections, and family units were routinely dissolved for economic gain.

"Miss Watson's Jim he was there, and I was going to help him escape. I knew the rules... but I couldn't let them trade him down the river."

Life Along the Mississippi River

1 / 2

The Primary National Artery

In the mid-19th century, the Mississippi River served as the dominant trade artery of the nation. Steamboats, cargo rafts, and transient river networks blended frontier settlements with bustling urban merchants.

"We said there warn't no home like a raft, after all. Other places do seem so cramped and smothery, but a raft don't."

West Wing — Hall A

The Legal Codes of Enslavement

1 / 5

The Chains of Legislation

State-enforced Slave Codes prohibited literacy education and denied basic human assembly, while federal statutes like the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 forced northern citizens to actively hunt and return escapees.

"I was a-trembling, because I'd got to decide, forever, betwixt two things, and I knowed it. I studied a minute... and says, 'All right, then, I'll go to hell!'"

West Wing — Hall B

Mark Twain: Author and Satirist

1 / 10

Samuel Clemens' River Pilot Days

Samuel Langhorne Clemens took his pen name from the depth-gauge safe call 'Mark Twain' (two fathoms deep). His years piloting steam vessels gave him direct exposure to the complex moral and social tensions of river society.

"The difference between the right word and the almost right word is the difference between lightning and a lightning bug."
